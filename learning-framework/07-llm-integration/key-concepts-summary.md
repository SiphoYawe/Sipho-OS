# Key Concepts Summary - Quick Reference for LLM Coaching

## Purpose
This document provides a rapid-access reference for LLM agents coaching students on effective learning. Use this as a first-pass lookup before diving into detailed framework sections.

---

## Core Philosophy (The Foundation)

### Pareto Principle Applied to Learning
- **Rule**: 20% of study methods yield 80% of results
- **Implication**: Focus effort on high-impact techniques
- **Anti-pattern**: Spending equal time on all activities
- **Coaching tip**: Help students identify and eliminate low-value activities

### Active vs Passive Learning
- **Active**: Retrieving, generating, applying information
- **Passive**: Reading, listening, watching without engagement
- **Evidence**: Active methods show 50-100% better retention
- **Key distinction**: Can you do this with your eyes closed? If yes, it's likely passive

### Efficiency Over Hours
- **Myth to bust**: "Study for X hours per day"
- **Reality**: Quality of engagement matters more than duration
- **Measure**: Information mastered per hour, not hours logged
- **Student translation**: "Work smarter, not longer"

### Desirable Difficulty
- **Concept**: Optimal challenge level maximizes learning
- **Too easy**: No neural adaptation (boredom)
- **Too hard**: Cognitive overload (frustration)
- **Sweet spot**: 70-85% success rate on practice attempts
- **Coaching application**: Adjust difficulty based on student performance

## Evidence-Based Techniques (Ranked by Effectiveness)

### 1. Active Recall / Practice Testing (★★★★★)
**Effect Size**: d = 0.80 (large)
**What**: Retrieving information from memory without looking at source material
**Why it works**: Forces memory consolidation, reveals gaps
**Implementation**:
- Close book, write everything you remember
- Use flashcards (properly)
- Practice problems without looking at solutions
- Teach concept to someone/something
**Time investment**: High effort, high return
**When to use**: After initial exposure to material, throughout learning process

### 2. Spaced Repetition (★★★★★)
**Effect Size**: d = 0.71 (large)
**What**: Reviewing material at increasing intervals
**Why it works**: Leverages spacing effect, combats forgetting curve
**Implementation**:
- Standard intervals: 1, 3, 7, 14, 30 days
- Adjust based on difficulty and importance
- Review BEFORE you forget completely
**Time investment**: Requires planning, huge long-term savings
**When to use**: For all material you need to remember long-term

### 3. Interleaved Practice (★★★★☆)
**Effect Size**: d = 0.65 (medium-large)
**What**: Mixing different topics/problem types in one session
**Why it works**: Improves discrimination, enhances transfer
**Implementation**:
- Don't do 20 problems of same type
- Rotate between topics every 10-15 minutes
- Mix old and new material
**Time investment**: Same time, better organization
**When to use**: Practice sessions, reviews, homework

### 4. Elaboration (★★★★☆)
**Effect Size**: d = 0.63 (medium-large)
**What**: Explaining and describing concepts in detail
**Why it works**: Creates rich memory associations
**Implementation**:
- Ask "why?" and "how?" questions
- Connect new info to existing knowledge
- Generate examples and applications
**Time investment**: Medium effort, high return
**When to use**: Initial learning, deepening understanding

### 5. Feynman Technique (★★★★☆)
**Combined approach**
**What**: Explain concept in simple terms as if teaching a beginner
**Why it works**: Exposes gaps, forces elaboration and simplification
**Implementation**:
1. Choose concept
2. Explain it simply (to rubber duck, paper, friend)
3. Identify gaps in explanation
4. Review and simplify further
**Time investment**: 15-30 minutes per concept
**When to use**: After reading, before exams, when confused

## Low Utility Methods (Avoid or Minimize)

### Re-reading (★☆☆☆☆)
**Effect Size**: d = 0.14 (negligible)
**Problem**: Creates fluency illusion
**Why students do it**: Feels easy, familiar
**What to do instead**: Active recall after first read
**Only useful if**: First reading was inadequate or unfocused

### Highlighting (★☆☆☆☆)
**Effect Size**: d = 0.09 (negligible)
**Problem**: Minimal processing, no retrieval practice
**Why students do it**: Feels productive, colorful
**What to do instead**: Write margin questions, create flashcards
**Only useful if**: Combined with elaboration and recall

### Summarizing (★★☆☆☆)
**Effect Size**: d = 0.26 (small)
**Problem**: Can be passive paraphrasing
**Why students do it**: Traditional advice
**What to do instead**: Create recall questions from summaries
**Can be useful if**: Done from memory, includes elaboration

### Passive Note-taking (★★☆☆☆)
**Problem**: Becomes transcription, not processing
**Why students do it**: Fear of missing information
**What to do instead**: Cornell method with recall column
**Red flag**: Writing verbatim what instructor says

## Productivity Systems

### Time Blocking
**Purpose**: Protect focus time, prevent task-switching
**Implementation**:
- Block calendar in advance
- Different blocks for different work types
- Include buffer time
**Template**:
```
08:00-10:00: Deep work (hardest subject)
10:00-10:15: Break
10:15-12:00: Active recall session
12:00-13:00: Lunch
13:00-14:30: Lighter tasks (reading, admin)
14:30-14:45: Break
14:45-16:00: Practice problems
```

### Pomodoro Technique
**Purpose**: Maintain focus, prevent burnout
**Standard format**: 25 min work / 5 min break
**Variations**:
- 50/10 for deep work
- 15/3 for difficult material
- 90/20 for flow states
**Critical**: True break (no phone scrolling, get up)

### 5-Minute Rule
**Purpose**: Overcome activation energy
**Implementation**: Commit to just 5 minutes
**Psychology**: Starting is hardest part, momentum carries forward
**Success rate**: ~80% continue past 5 minutes

### Peak Energy Windows
**Purpose**: Match task difficulty to energy levels
**Common patterns**:
- Morning (2-3 hours after waking): Peak cognitive function
- Post-lunch: Energy dip (good for routine tasks)
- Late afternoon: Secondary peak
- Evening: Wind-down (review, light practice)
**Coaching**: Help students identify their personal rhythms

## Key Formulas & Numbers

### Spaced Repetition Intervals
**Standard**: 1, 3, 7, 14, 30 days
**Intensive**: 1, 2, 4, 7, 14 days
**Long-term**: 7, 21, 45, 90 days
**Adjustment rule**: If you remember easily, extend next interval by 50%; if you struggle, reduce by 50%

### Pomodoro Ratios
**Standard**: 25 work / 5 break (5:1 ratio)
**Deep work**: 50 work / 10 break (5:1 ratio)
**Difficult material**: 15 work / 3 break (5:1 ratio)
**After 4 pomodoros**: Take 15-30 minute break

### Leitner Box System
**Box 1**: Daily review
**Box 2**: Every 3 days
**Box 3**: Weekly
**Box 4**: Bi-weekly
**Box 5**: Before exam only
**Rule**: Correct → move up; incorrect → back to Box 1

### Study Time Allocation
**Read/watch (passive)**: 20% of time
**Active recall**: 40% of time
**Practice problems**: 30% of time
**Review/spaced repetition**: 10% of time

### Forgetting Curve Milestones
**After 20 minutes**: Forget 40%
**After 24 hours**: Forget 70%
**After 1 week**: Forget 90%
**With review at 24h**: Retention increases to 80%

### Optimal Practice Difficulty
**Success rate target**: 70-85%
**If >90% correct**: Material too easy, increase difficulty
**If <60% correct**: Material too hard, break down further or review prerequisites

## Quick Diagnostic Questions

Use these to rapidly assess student situation:

### Study Method Assessment
1. "After you read/watch something, what do you do next?"
   - Looking for: Active recall mention
   - Red flag: "I re-read it" or "I highlight"

2. "How do you know if you've learned something?"
   - Looking for: Testing themselves
   - Red flag: "It feels familiar" or "I understand when I read"

3. "When do you review material you've already studied?"
   - Looking for: Scheduled intervals
   - Red flag: "Only before exams" or "When I have time"

### Time Management Assessment
4. "Describe a typical study session"
   - Looking for: Structured blocks, breaks, variety
   - Red flag: "Study until I finish" or vague descriptions

5. "What time of day do you study hardest material?"
   - Looking for: Awareness of energy patterns
   - Red flag: "Whenever I have time" or late night

### Progress Tracking Assessment
6. "How do you decide what to study today?"
   - Looking for: System based on performance/schedule
   - Red flag: "Whatever feels most urgent"

7. "Can you list topics you're confident vs struggling with?"
   - Looking for: Specific awareness
   - Red flag: General uncertainty

### Problem Identification
8. "How long do you study vs how much you feel you learn?"
   - Looking for: Efficiency concerns
   - Red flag: Long hours, little progress

9. "What happens when you try to recall material you studied?"
   - Looking for: Successful recall or specific gaps
   - Red flag: "I blank out" or "It's familiar but can't explain"

### Motivation Assessment
10. "What's the hardest part about studying for you?"
    - Looking for: Specific obstacles
    - Common answers: Starting, focusing, knowing what to do, staying motivated

## LLM Coaching Quick Start

When a student first approaches you:

### Step 1: Assess Current State (2-3 questions)
- What are they studying?
- What's their main challenge?
- What are they currently doing?

### Step 2: Identify Primary Issue
- **Ineffective methods** → Introduce active recall
- **Time/focus problems** → Implement productivity systems
- **Forgetting** → Set up spaced repetition
- **Overwhelmed** → Break down and prioritize
- **Procrastinating** → 5-minute rule + time blocking

### Step 3: Introduce ONE Change
- Don't overwhelm with entire framework
- Pick highest-impact intervention
- Give specific implementation steps
- Set up success criteria

### Step 4: Build From Success
- Once one technique working, add another
- Layer in more sophisticated approaches
- Customize to student's context

### Coaching Principles
1. **Meet students where they are**: Don't assume prior knowledge
2. **Contrast with current methods**: Show why change is needed
3. **Start small**: One technique, one subject, one week
4. **Make it concrete**: Not "use active recall" but "after reading, close book and write 10 things you remember"
5. **Emphasize evidence**: "Research shows..." builds credibility
6. **Celebrate wins**: Acknowledge when they try something new

## Common Student Profiles

### The Highlighter (Passive Learner)
- **Symptoms**: Long study hours, poor test performance, frustration
- **Diagnosis**: All passive methods (reading, highlighting, summarizing)
- **Intervention**: Introduce active recall immediately
- **Expected resistance**: "But this is how I've always studied"
- **Approach**: Small experiment, one chapter, measure results

### The Crammer
- **Symptoms**: Studies night before, forgets quickly
- **Diagnosis**: No spaced repetition, massed practice
- **Intervention**: Show forgetting curve, set up review schedule
- **Expected resistance**: "I don't have time to review"
- **Approach**: Show time saved by remembering vs re-learning

### The Perfectionist Procrastinator
- **Symptoms**: Delays starting, needs "perfect" conditions
- **Diagnosis**: Activation energy too high, all-or-nothing thinking
- **Intervention**: 5-minute rule, imperfect action beats perfect inaction
- **Expected resistance**: "I need to be in the right mindset"
- **Approach**: Reframe productivity as consistency, not intensity

### The Busy Spinner
- **Symptoms**: Always studying, going through motions, no improvement
- **Diagnosis**: Confusing activity with progress
- **Intervention**: Measure output (what can you recall/do?) not input (hours spent)
- **Expected resistance**: "But I'm working so hard!"
- **Approach**: Track specific mastery metrics

### The Lost Navigator
- **Symptoms**: Doesn't know what to study, unfocused effort
- **Diagnosis**: No system for prioritizing or tracking
- **Intervention**: Set up topic map with mastery ratings
- **Expected resistance**: "I need to study everything"
- **Approach**: Pareto principle, biggest gaps first

## Key Phrases for LLM Responses

### Introducing Active Recall
- "The single most important change: stop re-reading, start retrieving"
- "If you can't explain it without looking, you haven't learned it yet"
- "Your brain needs practice retrieving, not just storing"

### Explaining Spaced Repetition
- "Review before you forget completely, but not before you've started to forget"
- "Each successful recall makes the memory stronger and longer-lasting"
- "The effort of remembering is what creates learning"

### Addressing Resistance
- "Try this for one week on one subject, then compare results"
- "What you're doing now isn't working—what's the risk in trying something different?"
- "This feels harder because it IS harder—that's desirable difficulty"

### Building Motivation
- "You've just proven to yourself that you can recall this—that's real progress"
- "Most students never learn HOW to learn—you're building a superpower"
- "Each small win with these techniques builds your confidence"

### Promoting Efficiency
- "Learning faster means more free time, not more study time"
- "Work smarter so you can do what you actually enjoy"
- "The goal is mastery, not hours logged"

## Emergency Protocols

### Student Has Exam Tomorrow
1. Focus on highest-value material (Pareto: 20% = 80% of points)
2. Active recall on main concepts
3. Practice problems on most common question types
4. NO new material, only consolidate existing
5. Brief reviews with 1-2 hour spacing tonight
6. Good sleep > late-night cramming

### Student Completely Overwhelmed
1. Brain dump: list everything that needs to be done
2. Categorize by urgency and importance
3. Choose ONE thing to start (5-minute rule)
4. Break that one thing into smallest possible step
5. Complete that step, celebrate, repeat

### Student Failing Despite Effort
1. Audit study methods (usually passive)
2. Test actual recall ability (usually reveals gaps)
3. Implement active recall immediately
4. Set up office hours/tutoring for difficult material
5. Create practice problems before practicing

### Student Can't Focus/Constantly Distracted
1. Physical environment audit (phone, notifications, interruptions)
2. Implement Pomodoro (shorter cycles if needed)
3. Schedule breaks (prevent "I'll just check...")
4. One task per session (no multitasking)
5. Consider peak energy timing

## Success Metrics to Track

Help students measure these:

### Knowledge Metrics
- **Recall accuracy**: % correct on self-testing
- **Explanation fluency**: Can teach concept smoothly
- **Application success**: Solve new problems correctly
- **Speed of recall**: How quickly answers come

### Process Metrics
- **Active:passive time ratio**: Target 80:20
- **Review consistency**: Days reviewed vs days planned
- **Focus quality**: Pomodoros completed without distraction
- **Planning accuracy**: Estimated vs actual time needed

### Outcome Metrics
- **Test/exam scores**: Trending direction
- **Understanding confidence**: Self-rated, track over time
- **Time to mastery**: Decreasing per unit of material
- **Retention duration**: How long material stays accessible
