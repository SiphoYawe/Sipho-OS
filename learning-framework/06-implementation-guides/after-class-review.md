# After-Class Review: The Critical 20 Minutes

## Overview

This is the MOST IMPORTANT step in the entire learning system. The immediate after-class review is where memory consolidation happens. Skip this, and you lose 80% of the value from the lecture.

**Core Principle**: Your brain has a narrow window (1-2 hours) where information from lecture is still accessible but not yet consolidated. This is THE moment to practice retrieval and create lasting memory.

**Time Required**: 20 minutes immediately after class (or same day at latest)

**When**: Within 1-2 hours of lecture ending (non-negotiable)

**Goal**: Attempt to answer all questions from lecture, color-code understanding, identify gaps

---

## Why Immediate Review is Non-Negotiable

### The Forgetting Curve Without Review

**What happens if you DON'T review immediately**:

| Time After Lecture | Information Retained |
|-------------------|---------------------|
| Immediately after | ~90% |
| 1 hour later | ~70% |
| 1 day later | ~50% |
| 1 week later | ~20% |
| 1 month later | ~10% |

**By waiting just 24 hours, you've lost HALF the lecture.**

### The Forgetting Curve WITH Immediate Review

**What happens if you DO review immediately**:

| Time After Lecture | Action | Retention |
|-------------------|---------|-----------|
| Within 2 hours | Immediate review | ~95% |
| 1 day later | End-of-day review | ~90% |
| 1 week later | First spaced review | ~85% |
| 1 month later | Second spaced review | ~80% |

**Immediate review changes everything.**

### The Science

**Why the first 1-2 hours matter**:
- Information is in working memory (accessible but fragile)
- Retrieval practice NOW = transfer to long-term memory
- Synaptic consolidation happens in this window
- After 2+ hours, information has faded or been overwritten

**Critical finding**: One retrieval attempt within 2 hours = stronger memory than multiple study sessions later

**The research** (Bjork & Bjork):
- Testing immediately after learning ‚Üí 90% retention after 1 week
- No testing, studying later ‚Üí 40% retention after 1 week
- **More than 2x difference from 20 minutes of retrieval practice**

## The Complete Immediate Review Protocol

### Timing: When to Do This

**Ideal**: Immediately after lecture ends (like, before leaving the building)

**Acceptable**: Within 2 hours of lecture

**Minimum**: Same day as lecture

**Unacceptable**: Next day (too late, most benefit lost)

**How to ensure this happens**:
1. Schedule it as part of "class time" (lecture + review = one block)
2. Find spot near classroom (library, empty room, outdoor bench)
3. Set alarm reminder
4. Make it a non-negotiable habit (like brushing teeth)

**Example schedule**:
```
9:00-10:00 AM: Biology lecture
10:00-10:20 AM: Biology immediate review ‚Üê REQUIRED
10:20-10:30 AM: Break
10:30-11:30 AM: Next class
```

**Treat the 10:00-10:20 slot as MANDATORY.**

### Step 1: Go Through All Questions (10-12 minutes)

**What to do**:
1. Open your question-based notes from lecture
2. Go through each question in order
3. Attempt to answer FROM MEMORY (this is critical)
4. Do NOT look at textbook, slides, or any materials yet
5. Answer verbally (fastest) or write brief answer

**How to attempt answers**:
- **Verbal**: Say answer out loud (or in your head)
- **Written**: Write 1-3 sentence answer
- **Mixed**: Speak for most, write for complex ones

**Key principle**: STRUGGLE with retrieval

**What struggling looks like**:
- "Hmm, I remember the professor said something about... was it...?"
- "I know this has to do with X, but I can't remember the exact mechanism"
- "There were three steps, I remember the first two..."

**This struggle IS the learning.** Don't skip to the answer too quickly.

**Example** (going through Biology questions):

```
Question 1: What is photosynthesis?
[Attempt from memory]: "Photosynthesis is the process plants use
to convert light energy into chemical energy... they use sunlight,
water, and CO2 to make glucose and oxygen."
[Assessment]: I got this ‚úì

Question 2: Where in the cell does photosynthesis occur?
[Attempt from memory]: "In the chloroplast... there are two parts,
the thylakoid and the... stroma I think?"
[Assessment]: Partial, not confident about stroma

Question 3: What are the two main stages of photosynthesis?
[Attempt from memory]: "Light-dependent and light-independent...
or are they called light and dark reactions? Calvin cycle is one?"
[Assessment]: Confused about naming

Question 4: What happens in light-dependent reactions?
[Attempt from memory]: "Um... something with water being split?
And making ATP? I don't remember the details."
[Assessment]: Very weak on this

[Continue through all questions...]
```

**Benchmark**: 20-30 seconds per question attempt = 10-12 minutes for 20-40 questions

### Step 2: Color Code Based on Understanding (3-4 minutes)

**What to do**:
1. Go back through each question
2. Assign a color based on how well you answered
3. Use consistent color system (create legend)

**The Color-Coding System**:

| Color | Meaning | What This Looks Like |
|-------|---------|---------------------|
| **Black** | Answered correctly with detail | Full, confident answer with specifics |
| **Blue** | Answered partially or with uncertainty | Got main idea but missed details/nuance |
| **Purple** | Couldn't answer at all | Drew blank or totally wrong answer |

**Alternative color system** (if you prefer):
- **Green** = Solid understanding
- **Yellow** = Partial understanding
- **Orange** = Weak understanding
- **Red** = No understanding

**Choose one system and stick with it for entire semester.**

**How to decide which color**:

**Black/Green criteria** (all must be true):
- [ ] Could answer without long pause
- [ ] Answer was accurate and detailed
- [ ] Felt confident about answer
- [ ] Could teach this to someone else

**Blue/Yellow criteria** (some true):
- [ ] Got general idea but missed specifics
- [ ] Needed time to think/remember
- [ ] Answer was partially correct
- [ ] Some uncertainty or confusion

**Purple/Red criteria** (any true):
- [ ] Couldn't remember at all
- [ ] Drew complete blank
- [ ] Answer was entirely wrong
- [ ] High confusion about concept

**Example** (color coding same questions):

```
Question 1: What is photosynthesis? [BLACK]
Question 2: Where in cell does photosynthesis occur? [BLUE]
Question 3: What are two main stages? [BLUE]
Question 4: What happens in light-dependent reactions? [PURPLE]
Question 5: What happens in light-independent reactions? [PURPLE]
Question 6: What are products of photosynthesis? [BLACK]
Question 7: What are inputs needed? [BLACK]
Question 8: How do two stages connect? [PURPLE]
```

**Be brutally honest with yourself.**

**Common mistake**: Coding everything black/green because you "kind of" understand
**Result**: False confidence, poor exam performance

**Better**: Over-code purple/blue (if unsure, mark it lower)
**Result**: Targeted review, actual mastery

---

### Step 3: Reformat/Rewrite Questions for Clarity (2-3 minutes)

**What to do**:
1. Review your questions from lecture
2. Some will be unclear, abbreviated, or incomplete
3. Rewrite them for future study

**Example reformatting**:

**During lecture** (rushed, abbreviated):
```
- mitosis phases??
- DNA -> RNA???
- enzyme temp thing
```

**After-class review** (reformulated):
```
- What are the phases of mitosis in correct order? [PURPLE]
- What is the process of DNA being transcribed to RNA? [BLUE]
- How does temperature affect enzyme activity? [BLUE]
```

**Why this matters**:
- Clear questions = effective future study
- Vague questions = wasted time later trying to remember what you meant
- Reformulating forces you to think about the concept again (more encoding)

**Quick test**: "If I read this question in 2 weeks, would I know what I'm asking?"
- If yes: Leave as is
- If no: Reformulate now

---

### Step 4: Cover Diagrams and Attempt to Recall (2 minutes)

**What to do**:
1. For any diagrams in slides or textbook mentioned in lecture
2. Cover the diagram with hand/paper
3. Try to draw it from memory (rough sketch OK)
4. Uncover and compare
5. Color code your diagram recall

**Example** (Cell membrane structure):
```
Step 1: Cover Figure 5.3 (cell membrane diagram)
Step 2: Draw what you remember (phospholipid bilayer, proteins)
Step 3: Uncover and compare
Step 4: Color code:
   - If you got structure mostly right: BLACK
   - If you got general idea but wrong details: BLUE
   - If you couldn't draw it: PURPLE
```

**Why diagrams need special attention**:
- Visual memory is different from verbal memory
- "I recognized it" ‚â† "I can recall it"
- Diagrams often appear on exams
- Drawing forces deeper encoding

**Pro tip**: Use your phone to take pictures of important diagrams during lecture (2 seconds), then practice recalling them during review

---

### Step 5: Prioritize for End-of-Day Review (1 minute)

**What to do**:
1. Count how many purple, blue, and black questions you have
2. Mark the top 5-10 purple/blue questions with a star (‚≠ê)
3. These are your priority for end-of-day review

**Selection criteria for starred questions**:
- Professor emphasized this (said "important" or "on exam")
- Foundational concept (needed to understand later topics)
- You're very confused about it
- Appeared in pretest (you've now struggled with it twice)

**Example**:
```
Total questions: 28
Purple: 8
Blue: 12
Black: 8

Top priority (starred):
‚≠ê What is the electron transport chain? [PURPLE]
‚≠ê How does NADH produce ATP? [PURPLE]
‚≠ê What's the total ATP yield from glucose? [BLUE]
‚≠ê Why is oxygen needed for cellular respiration? [BLUE]
‚≠ê What happens in Krebs cycle? [PURPLE]

These 5 get extra attention tonight.
```

### Step 6: Optional - Consult Materials for Purple Questions (5 minutes)

**Note**: This step is OPTIONAL for immediate review. Can be saved for end-of-day review.

**What to do** (if you have time):
1. For questions you colored PURPLE
2. NOW look at textbook, slides, or notes
3. Find the answer
4. Write a brief answer in your notes
5. Try to answer again from memory
6. If successful, change color from PURPLE ‚Üí BLUE

**Example**:
```
Question: What is the electron transport chain? [PURPLE]

[Look up in textbook Chapter 9.4]

Answer found: "Series of protein complexes in inner mitochondrial
membrane that transfer electrons from NADH/FADH2 to oxygen,
creating proton gradient that drives ATP synthesis"

[Cover answer, try to explain in own words]

My explanation: "Proteins in mitochondria that pass electrons
along and use that to make ATP by creating a proton gradient"

[Close enough - change to BLUE]
```

**Why OPTIONAL**:
- The struggle (attempting to recall) is more important than getting the answer
- Looking up answers can create false sense of mastery
- Better to struggle again during end-of-day review (spacing effect)
- Only look up if you have extra time or are very confused

**When to include this step**:
- Light day with only 1-2 lectures
- Topic is critically important
- You're very confused and anxious about it
- Exam is coming up soon

**When to skip this step**:
- Heavy day with 3+ lectures
- You'll have time for end-of-day review
- Want to maximize spacing effect

## The End-of-Day Review (20 minutes before bed)

This is the SECOND review on the same day. Critical for spacing effect.

**When**: Before bed (or end of your day)

**What to do**:
1. Review ALL questions from today's lectures
2. Focus especially on starred (‚≠ê) purple/blue questions
3. Attempt to answer again FROM MEMORY
4. NOW consult textbook/slides for questions you still can't answer
5. Update color coding if improvement

**Why before bed**:
- Sleep consolidates memory
- Reviewing before sleep = enhanced consolidation
- Spacing (morning review + evening review) = stronger encoding

**Protocol**:

```
Step 1: Quick pass through ALL questions (10 min)
- Attempt each question verbally from memory
- Note which ones you can NOW answer (benefit of spacing)
- Don't write full answers (just check your recall)

Step 2: Deep focus on starred questions (8 min)
- For each starred question:
  1. Attempt to answer
  2. Consult textbook/slides if needed
  3. Write or speak complete answer
  4. Cover and try again
  5. Update color code

Step 3: Final check (2 min)
- Flip through all questions
- Visual review of topics covered
- Mental summary: "What did I learn today?"
```

**Example end-of-day review**:

```
Biology questions from 9 AM class:

Quick pass:
- Questions 1-3: Can answer (BLACK still)
- Question 4: NOW can partially answer! (PURPLE ‚Üí BLUE)
- Questions 5-7: Can answer (BLACK still)
- Question 8: Still can't answer (PURPLE still)
- [Continue...]

Starred questions deep dive:
‚≠ê What is electron transport chain? [PURPLE]
[Attempt from memory]: "I remember now, it's proteins that pass
electrons and create a gradient for ATP..."
[Check textbook]: Yes! Got the main idea
[Update color]: PURPLE ‚Üí BLUE ‚úì

‚≠ê How does NADH produce ATP? [PURPLE]
[Attempt from memory]: "NADH donates electrons to the electron
transport chain, which... creates a proton gradient? And that
powers ATP synthase?"
[Check textbook]: Correct!
[Update color]: PURPLE ‚Üí BLUE ‚úì

[Continue for all starred questions...]
```

**Goal**: Purple ‚Üí Blue ‚Üí Black progression

## Handling Multiple Classes in One Day

### 3+ Lectures in One Day

**Challenge**: 3 lectures = 60+ questions = 60 minutes of immediate review

**Solutions**:

**Option 1: Abbreviated immediate review**
- 10 minutes per class (instead of 20)
- Quick attempt at all questions
- Color code only
- Full review saved for end-of-day

**Option 2: Prioritized immediate review**
- Full 20-minute review for hardest class
- 10-minute review for medium class
- 5-minute review for easy class

**Option 3: Rolling review**
- Review Class 1 between Class 1 and Class 2
- Review Class 2 between Class 2 and Class 3
- Review Class 3 after Class 3
- End-of-day: Quick pass through all

**Recommended**: Option 3 (rolling review)

**Example schedule** (3 lectures):
```
9:00-10:00 AM: Biology
10:00-10:20 AM: Biology immediate review
10:30-11:30 AM: Chemistry
11:30-11:50 AM: Chemistry immediate review
12:00-1:00 PM: Physics
1:00-1:20 PM: Physics immediate review
[Evening]
9:00-9:30 PM: End-of-day review (all 3 classes)
```

### Back-to-Back Classes

**Challenge**: Two lectures with no break in between

**Solutions**:

**Option 1: Combined review**
- Both lectures end
- 30-minute review block (15 min each)

**Option 2: Delayed but same-day**
- Review after lunch or later
- MUST be same day

**Option 3: Between-class micro-review**
- 5-minute break between classes
- Quick color-coding only
- Full review later

**Example**:
```
10:00-11:00 AM: Class A
11:00-12:00 PM: Class B (back-to-back)
12:00-12:40 PM: Lunch + review both classes
```

## Common Challenges and Solutions

### Challenge 1: "I don't have time immediately after class"

**Reframe**: You don't have time NOT to do this.

**Reality check**:
- Skipping immediate review = need 2-3x more study time later
- 20 minutes now saves 60 minutes later
- This IS the most efficient use of time

**Solutions if genuinely constrained**:
1. **Micro-review**: 5-10 minutes (bare minimum)
   - Just color-code questions (attempt to answer verbally only)
   - Full review saved for end-of-day
2. **Reprioritize**: What are you doing instead?
   - Scrolling phone? ‚Üí Do review instead
   - Chatting with friends? ‚Üí 15-minute chat, then review
   - Rushing to lunch? ‚Üí Review while eating
3. **Schedule protection**: Block this time in calendar as non-negotiable

**Hard truth**: If you consistently "don't have time" for 20-minute review, you're setting yourself up for poor grades and stressed cramming later.

### Challenge 2: "I can't answer ANY questions (100% purple)"

**First**: Is this first lecture on brand new topic?
- If yes: Some purple is expected
- But if EVERYTHING is purple, something's wrong

**Possible causes**:
1. **Didn't do pretest**: Start pretesting (primes your brain)
2. **Didn't pay attention in lecture**: Improve focus
3. **Questions too hard**: Break into simpler questions
4. **Material genuinely very difficult**: Seek help NOW

**Immediate actions**:
1. Attend professor office hours THIS WEEK
2. Form study group
3. Watch supplementary videos (Khan Academy, etc.)
4. Consider tutoring if this continues

**Don't wait**: 100% purple after first lecture is an emergency. Address immediately.

### Challenge 3: "I code everything black but do poorly on exams"

**This means**: You're being too generous with yourself (or testing doesn't match studying)

**Solutions**:
1. **Raise the bar for BLACK**:
   - Can you teach this to someone who's never learned it?
   - Can you apply it in new contexts?
   - Can you answer related questions not explicitly asked?
2. **Test yourself more rigorously**:
   - Don't just recognize answer ("oh yeah, that sounds right")
   - Actually speak/write complete answer
   - Cover your written answer and try again
3. **Compare to exam questions**:
   - If exam asks application but you only tested definition ‚Üí not actually BLACK

**Recalibration**:
- After first exam, review what you coded BLACK
- If you missed those questions ‚Üí your BLACK bar is too low
- Adjust for next round

### Challenge 4: "This takes longer than 20 minutes"

**Possible causes**:
1. Too many questions (wrote 50+ during lecture)
2. Trying to write full answers (should be brief attempts)
3. Looking up every answer (save most for end-of-day)
4. Perfecting questions instead of color-coding quickly

**Solutions**:
1. **Limit lecture questions**: 20-40 max (quality over quantity)
2. **Verbal answers**: Speak answers instead of writing (much faster)
3. **Delay lookup**: Only look up a few critical purples, rest later
4. **Use timer**: Set 20-minute timer, finish when it beeps

**Time breakdown** (for 30 questions):
- 10 min: Quick attempt at each question (20 sec each)
- 5 min: Color code all questions
- 3 min: Reformulate unclear questions
- 2 min: Diagram recall practice

**Total**: 20 minutes

**If still over**: Do abbreviated version (better than skipping entirely)

### Challenge 5: "I feel like I'm not learning because I can't answer questions"

**Reframe**: Struggling to answer IS learning.

**Key concept**: Retrieval practice works BECAUSE it's difficult
- Easy recall = weak memory strengthening
- Difficult recall = strong memory strengthening
- Failed recall = priming for future learning

**Research finding**: Testing and failing STILL produces better learning than re-studying

**What to expect**:
- Immediate review: Lots of purple (can't answer)
- End-of-day review: Some purple ‚Üí blue
- Spaced review 3 days later: Some blue ‚Üí black
- Spaced review 1 week later: Most questions black

**The progression takes time.** Trust the process.

### Challenge 6: "Material from lecture was too much to review"

**This suggests**:
1. Lecture covered too much (common in survey courses)
2. You wrote too many questions
3. Questions need better prioritization

**Solutions**:
1. **Selective review**: Review only ‚≠ê marked questions in immediate review
2. **Categorize during lecture**: Mark questions as High/Medium/Low priority in real-time
3. **Abbreviate for low-priority**: Just color-code, don't attempt full answers
4. **Accept imperfection**: Cover 80% thoroughly > 100% superficially

**Priority markers to use during lecture**:
- ‚≠ê = Professor said "important"
- EXAM = Professor said "on exam"
- üîë = Foundational concept
- Regular = Standard content

**In review**: Focus on ‚≠ê, EXAM, üîë questions first

## Advanced Immediate Review Techniques

### Technique 1: Comparison Review

**What**: Compare today's material to previous lectures

**How**:
1. After color-coding questions, add 2-3 comparison questions:
   - "How is today's topic different from last lecture?"
   - "What connects today's material to previous topics?"
   - "How has this concept built on what we learned before?"
2. Attempt to answer these connection questions
3. This builds integrated knowledge (not isolated topics)

**Example**:
```
Today: Covalent bonding
Previous: Ionic bonding

Comparison questions:
- How is covalent bonding different from ionic bonding? [BLUE]
- In what situations does covalent vs ionic bonding occur? [PURPLE]
- What do both types of bonding accomplish? [BLACK]
```

### Technique 2: Teach-Back Review

**What**: Explain concepts out loud as if teaching someone

**How**:
1. Pick 3-5 questions from your list
2. Speak complete answer out loud (record yourself if helpful)
3. Use plain language, no jargon
4. If you can teach it, it's BLACK
5. If you struggle, it's BLUE or PURPLE

**Why this works**: Teaching requires deepest level of understanding

**Example**:
```
Question: What is natural selection?

Teach-back: "Imagine I'm explaining to my friend who's not in this class...
Natural selection is when organisms with traits that help them survive
are more likely to reproduce and pass those traits to offspring. Over many
generations, helpful traits become more common in the population while
unhelpful traits disappear. It's like... if rabbits that can run fast avoid
predators better, they live longer and have more baby rabbits, and those
babies inherit the fast-running genes."

[Assessment]: I could explain this clearly ‚Üí BLACK
```

### Technique 3: Predictive Review

**What**: Predict what will come next

**How**:
1. After reviewing today's questions, write:
   - "What topic will likely come next lecture?"
   - "What questions do I have going forward?"
   - "What will we probably build on from today?"
2. This primes your brain for next lecture

**Example**:
```
Today we learned: Mitosis (cell division)
Prediction for next lecture: Meiosis (specialized cell division)

Questions for next lecture:
- How is meiosis different from mitosis?
- Why do we need two types of cell division?
- What happens in meiosis specifically?

I'll pretest these before next class.
```

### Technique 4: Error Analysis Review

**What**: For PURPLE questions, identify WHY you couldn't answer

**How**:
1. For each purple question, note:
   - "Never heard this concept before"
   - "Forgot during lecture"
   - "Confused it with something else"
   - "Understand concept but not details"
2. This identifies patterns in your gaps

**Example**:
```
PURPLE questions analysis:

Q4: What is oxidative phosphorylation? [Never heard this term]
Q8: How many ATP from one glucose? [Forgot the numbers]
Q12: What's the difference between NADH and FADH2? [Confused them]
Q15: Where does Krebs cycle occur? [Know the process, forgot location]

Pattern: I'm weak on terminology and specific details, but understand
general processes.

Action: Focus vocabulary study and memorization of specifics.
```

### Technique 5: Multi-Modal Review

**What**: Use different senses and modalities

**How**:
1. **Verbal**: Speak answers out loud
2. **Written**: Write answers on paper
3. **Visual**: Draw diagrams or concept maps
4. **Kinesthetic**: Act out processes with hand gestures
5. **Auditory**: Record yourself explaining and listen back

**Example** (photosynthesis review):
```
Verbal: Explain photosynthesis out loud
Written: Write steps of light-dependent reactions
Visual: Draw chloroplast structure and label parts
Kinesthetic: Use hands to show electron flow
Auditory: Record explanation and listen while walking

Each modality strengthens different neural pathways.
```

## Integration with Spaced Repetition

### How Immediate Review Fits in Spacing Schedule

**The complete review schedule**:

| Timing | Review Type | Duration | Purpose |
|--------|-------------|----------|---------|
| Within 2 hours | Immediate review | 20 min | Initial encoding |
| Same evening | End-of-day review | 20 min | First spacing interval |
| 3 days later | First spaced review | 15 min | Combat forgetting |
| 1 week later | Second spaced review | 15 min | Long-term consolidation |
| 2 weeks later | Third spaced review | 10 min | Exam prep begins |
| 1 month later | Fourth spaced review | 10 min | Cumulative review |

**Immediate review is the FOUNDATION.**

Without it, the spacing schedule is building on sand.

### Using Color Codes to Guide Spacing

**Spaced review protocol** (3 days later):

1. Review ONLY purple and blue questions (skip black)
2. Attempt to answer from memory
3. Update color codes
4. Questions that moved BLACK ‚Üí don't review again until next spacing interval
5. Questions still PURPLE ‚Üí need different study approach

**Example** (3 days after lecture):

```
Original immediate review colors:
BLACK: 8 questions
BLUE: 12 questions
PURPLE: 8 questions

Spaced review (3 days later):
- Review the 20 BLUE + PURPLE questions
- Skip the 8 BLACK questions

Results after spaced review:
- 5 PURPLE ‚Üí BLUE (improvement!)
- 6 BLUE ‚Üí BLACK (mastered!)
- 3 PURPLE still PURPLE (need help on these)
- 6 BLUE still BLUE (need more practice)

New totals:
BLACK: 14 questions (8 + 6)
BLUE: 11 questions (12 - 6 + 5)
PURPLE: 3 questions (8 - 5)

Progress: 50% of material now mastered (BLACK)
```

**This data guides next study session.**

## Final Implementation Guidance

### The First Time Will Feel Awkward

**What to expect**:
- Uncomfortable retrieving (you'll struggle a lot)
- Surprising how much you've forgotten (after just 1 hour!)
- Lots of purple coding (discouraging at first)
- Feels like you learned nothing (this is the point)

**Why this is good**:
- Struggling = your brain encoding
- Forgotten material = exactly what needs review
- Purple coding = honest assessment
- "Learned nothing" = you're identifying gaps (that's learning)

**After 3-4 immediate reviews**: You'll notice you remember MORE from lectures than you used to. This is the evidence it's working.

### Make It Non-Negotiable

**Mental frame**: Immediate review is PART of the lecture, not separate.

**Schedule it as**:
```
9:00-10:20 AM: Biology (60 min lecture + 20 min review)
```

NOT as:
```
9:00-10:00 AM: Biology
[Maybe review later if I have time]
```

**Treat the review block like you treat the lecture itself**: You wouldn't skip the last 20 minutes of lecture, don't skip the 20-minute review.
