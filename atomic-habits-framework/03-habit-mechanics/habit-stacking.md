# Habit Stacking: Building New Habits on Existing Routines

## Overview

Habit stacking is a strategy for building new habits by anchoring them to existing behaviors. Instead of starting from scratch with cues like time or location, you use the completion of one habit as the trigger for the next. This leverages existing neural pathways and creates powerful chains of behavior that compound throughout your day.

---

## The Diderot Effect: The Foundation of Habit Stacking

### Origin Story

**Denis Diderot** (1765): French philosopher receives a beautiful scarlet robe as a gift.

**The cascade**:
1. New robe looks too elegant for his current study
2. Replaces study furniture to match robe's elegance
3. New furniture makes old paintings look shabby
4. Replaces art to match new aesthetic
5. Continues replacing possessions to maintain consistency
6. Eventually in debt from this cascade of purchases

**His reflection**: "I was absolute master of my old robe, but I have become a slave to my new one."

### The Principle

**Core insight**: One behavior naturally leads to another. Obtaining a new possession often creates a spiral of consumption. More importantly for habits: completing one behavior creates momentum for the next.

**Original context**: Consumer behavior and lifestyle creep

**Applied to habits**: One behavior can serve as the trigger for another, creating chains of actions that flow naturally.

### The Psychological Mechanism

**Pattern completion**: The human brain seeks closure and consistency. Starting one action creates psychological momentum to complete related actions.

**Neural clustering**: Related behaviors stored near each other in procedural memory activate more easily in sequence.

**Cognitive fluidity**: Once in motion, continuing is easier than starting. The first habit breaks inertia for subsequent habits.

**Examples in daily life**:
- Washing hands → Drying hands → Hanging towel (automatic sequence)
- Pouring coffee → Adding cream → Adding sugar (linked behaviors)
- Getting in car → Putting on seatbelt → Starting engine (safety chain)
- Finishing meal → Clearing plate → Washing dishes (cleanup routine)

## The Habit Stacking Formula

### Basic Structure

**Format**: "After [CURRENT HABIT], I will [NEW HABIT]"

**Components**:
1. **Anchor habit**: Existing behavior you already do automatically
2. **Linking phrase**: "After..." creates the connection
3. **New habit**: Behavior you want to build

**Why it works**:
- Uses existing neural pathway as foundation
- Eliminates need to remember or rely on time-based cues
- Creates immediate, obvious trigger
- Leverages completion energy of first habit

### Essential Requirements

**The anchor habit must be**:
1. **Already automatic**: Not something you're also trying to build
2. **Specific**: Precise behavior, not vague category
3. **Consistent**: Happens at same frequency as desired new habit
4. **Obvious**: Completion point is clear
5. **Small enough**: Doesn't create barrier to new habit

### Examples Across Domains

**Physical health**:
- "After I pour my morning coffee, I will do 10 pushups"
- "After I brush my teeth at night, I will floss one tooth"
- "After I take off my work shoes, I will change into workout clothes"
- "After I sit down for lunch, I will eat one serving of vegetables first"

**Mental health**:
- "After I sit down at my desk, I will write three things I'm grateful for"
- "After I close my laptop for the day, I will do 2 minutes of meditation"
- "After I get into bed, I will read one page of a book"
- "After I feel stressed, I will take three deep breaths"

**Productivity**:
- "After I start my computer, I will write my top three priorities"
- "After I finish a meeting, I will send follow-up notes"
- "After I complete a work task, I will update my task list"
- "After I answer an email, I will immediately file or delete it"

**Relationships**:
- "After I walk in the door from work, I will hug my partner"
- "After I sit down for dinner, I will ask one question about my child's day"
- "After I drink my morning coffee, I will text one friend"
- "After I finish work on Friday, I will schedule one social activity for the weekend"

**Learning**:
- "After I pour my morning coffee, I will read one page of a non-fiction book"
- "After I eat lunch, I will review my language flashcards for 5 minutes"
- "After I turn off my car, I will listen to one podcast episode summary"
- "After I brush my teeth in the morning, I will practice the piano for 2 minutes"

## Building Chains of Habits

### Single Stack vs. Chain Stack

**Single stack**:
- One trigger → One new habit
- Example: "After I pour coffee, I will meditate for 1 minute"

**Chain stack**:
- Multiple habits linked in sequence
- Example: Coffee → Meditation → Journaling → Exercise → Shower
- Creates powerful routines from individual behaviors

### Constructing Effective Chains

**Principle**: Link habits that naturally flow together based on location, energy level, or logical sequence.

**Framework**:
1. Start with existing anchor
2. Add first new habit
3. Let first new habit become anchor for second new habit
4. Continue building chain
5. Keep each habit small enough that chain remains doable

**Formula for chains**:
```
After [EXISTING HABIT], I will [NEW HABIT 1]
After [NEW HABIT 1], I will [NEW HABIT 2]
After [NEW HABIT 2], I will [NEW HABIT 3]
```

### Chain Examples

**Morning routine chain**:
```
1. After my alarm goes off, I will immediately sit up
2. After I sit up, I will drink a glass of water on my nightstand
3. After I drink water, I will make my bed
4. After I make my bed, I will put on my workout clothes
5. After I put on workout clothes, I will do 5 minutes of stretching
6. After I finish stretching, I will meditate for 2 minutes
7. After I meditate, I will write three priorities for the day
8. After I write my priorities, I will make breakfast
9. After I make breakfast, I will review my calendar while eating
10. After I finish breakfast, I will brush my teeth and start my day
```

**Evening wind-down chain**:
```
1. After I finish dinner, I will clear my plate
2. After I clear my plate, I will wash the dishes
3. After I wash dishes, I will wipe down the counters
4. After I wipe counters, I will set out coffee for tomorrow
5. After I set out coffee, I will dim the lights
6. After I dim lights, I will change into comfortable clothes
7. After I change clothes, I will set out tomorrow's outfit
8. After I set out clothes, I will pack my bag for tomorrow
9. After I pack my bag, I will turn off all screens
10. After I turn off screens, I will read for 20 minutes
11. After I read, I will brush my teeth
12. After I brush teeth, I will get into bed
```

**Work focus chain**:
```
1. After I arrive at my desk, I will close all browser tabs
2. After I close tabs, I will put phone in drawer
3. After I put phone away, I will review my task list
4. After I review tasks, I will identify my #1 priority
5. After I identify priority, I will set a 25-minute timer
6. After I set timer, I will start working on priority task
7. After timer goes off, I will take a 5-minute break
8. After my break, I will check for urgent messages
9. After checking messages, I will reset timer and continue
```

### Benefits of Chain Stacking

**Momentum compounding**:
- First habit breaks inertia
- Each subsequent habit is easier than starting from scratch
- Completion energy carries through entire chain

**Reduced decision fatigue**:
- No decisions between habits
- Entire sequence becomes single automated routine
- Willpower required only to start chain, not for each habit

**Stronger neural encoding**:
- Multiple repetitions in close succession
- Brain chunks entire sequence together
- Becomes single procedural memory unit

**Time efficiency**:
- Transitions between habits are seamless
- No "what should I do next?" dead time
- Can accomplish significant routine in short period

**Psychological satisfaction**:
- Checking off multiple habits creates momentum
- Sense of accomplishment amplifies
- Identity reinforcement: "I'm the type of person who has powerful routines"

### Dangers and Limitations of Long Chains

**Fragility**:
- Breaking chain at any point can disrupt entire sequence
- One missed habit can derail routine
- Creates all-or-nothing mentality

**Overwhelm**:
- Too many habits can feel daunting
- May avoid starting because chain seems too long
- Initial friction increases with chain length

**Inflexibility**:
- Life disruptions break rigid chains
- Travel, illness, schedule changes affect entire routine
- Can create guilt when unable to complete full chain

**Mitigation strategies**:
1. Keep chains to 3-5 habits maximum initially
2. Build optional "flex points" where chain can branch
3. Create minimum viable version: core habits that must happen vs. bonus habits
4. Allow interruptions without self-judgment
5. Have multiple shorter chains rather than one mega-chain

## Using Existing Neural Pathways as Anchors

### Why Existing Habits Make Perfect Triggers

**Neural efficiency**: Pathways already established and myelinated, signals travel automatically

**Consistency**: Habits you already do reliably provide reliable triggers

**No additional cognitive load**: Don't need to remember new cue or set up new system

**Automatic activation**: Completion of existing habit creates natural moment for new behavior

### Identifying Your Existing Habits

**Exercise**: List all behaviors you do every day without thinking

**Categories to consider**:
- Wake up and sleep routines
- Bathroom and hygiene
- Meal preparation and eating
- Commute and transportation
- Work start and end rituals
- Technology use patterns
- Social interactions
- Household maintenance

**Common daily habits most people have**:
- Wake up, get out of bed
- Use bathroom
- Brush teeth (morning and night)
- Shower/bathe
- Get dressed
- Make/pour coffee or tea
- Eat breakfast
- Eat lunch
- Eat dinner
- Check phone
- Check email
- Arrive at work/desk
- Leave work
- Return home
- Change clothes
- Prepare for bed
- Get into bed
- Turn off lights

**Your existing habits are your foundation** - build new behaviors onto this existing structure.

### Matching New Habits to Appropriate Anchors

**Considerations**:

1. **Frequency alignment**
   - Daily habit needs daily anchor
   - Weekly habit needs weekly anchor
   - Example: Don't anchor "weekly review" to "brushing teeth"

2. **Location compatibility**
   - Anchor and new habit should occur in same/adjacent location
   - Example: Don't anchor "go for run" to "check email at desk"

3. **Energy level matching**
   - High-energy habits need high-energy anchors
   - Low-energy habits work with low-energy anchors
   - Example: Don't anchor "intense workout" to "getting into bed"

4. **Logical flow**
   - Habits should make sense in sequence
   - Natural progression from one to next
   - Example: "After I shower, I will moisturize" (logical flow)

5. **Time availability**
   - Ensure gap after anchor is sufficient for new habit
   - Example: Don't anchor "30-minute meditation" to "grabbing keys before leaving"

### Strong vs. Weak Anchors

**Strong anchors** (Highly recommended):
- Very specific: "After I pour my morning coffee" not "After I wake up"
- Highly consistent: Happens same way every single time
- Obvious completion point: Clear when anchor is done
- Examples:
  - "After I close my laptop at end of day..."
  - "After I plug in my phone to charge..."
  - "After I sit down at my desk..."
  - "After I park my car..."

**Weak anchors** (Avoid):
- Vague: "After I get ready in the morning..."
- Inconsistent: "After I check social media..." (varies wildly)
- No clear endpoint: "After I relax..."
- Examples to avoid:
  - "After I feel like it..."
  - "After I have some free time..."
  - "After I finish working..." (when exactly?)
  - "After I eat..." (which meal? when finished eating?)

### Advanced Anchoring Techniques

**State-based anchors** (for habits addressing emotional states):
- "After I notice I'm feeling stressed, I will take 3 deep breaths"
- "After I feel bored, I will do 10 squats"
- "After I notice myself procrastinating, I will work for just 2 minutes"

**Conditional anchors** (if-then planning):
- "If it's Monday, after I wake up, I will meal prep"
- "If I'm feeling low energy, after lunch, I will take a 10-minute walk"
- "If I completed my priority task, after I finish, I will take a real break"

**Location-transition anchors**:
- "After I walk through my front door, I will hang up my keys"
- "After I sit down at my desk, I will close all browser tabs"
- "After I get in my car, I will put phone in center console"

**Person-based anchors**:
- "After I see my child after school, I will ask about their day"
- "After I greet my partner in the morning, I will give them a hug"
- "After I meet with my manager, I will update my task list"

## Morning Routine Stacking Examples

### Minimal Morning Stack (5 minutes)

**Foundation**: For people with limited morning time

```
After my alarm goes off → I will immediately sit up (don't allow snooze)
After I sit up → I will drink water from bedside glass
After I drink water → I will make my bed
After I make my bed → I will put on workout clothes
After I put on clothes → I will do 10 pushups
```

**Total time**: ~5 minutes
**Benefits**: Wakes you up, creates immediate wins, builds momentum for day

### Standard Morning Stack (20 minutes)

**Foundation**: For people with moderate morning time

```
After my alarm goes off → I will sit up and drink bedside water
After I drink water → I will make my bed
After I make my bed → I will open the blinds
After I open blinds → I will do 5 minutes of stretching
After I stretch → I will meditate for 2 minutes
After I meditate → I will write 3 priorities for the day
After I write priorities → I will make coffee
After coffee starts brewing → I will take a quick shower
After I shower → I will get dressed
After I get dressed → I will drink my coffee while reviewing calendar
After I review calendar → I will eat a healthy breakfast
After breakfast → I will brush teeth and leave
```

**Total time**: ~20-25 minutes
**Benefits**: Comprehensive routine touching mind, body, planning

### Comprehensive Morning Stack (60 minutes)

**Foundation**: For people who prioritize extensive morning routine

```
After my alarm at 6:00 AM → I will turn on lights and sit up
After I sit up → I will drink full glass of water with lemon
After I drink water → I will make my bed immediately
After I make bed → I will put on workout clothes
After I put on clothes → I will do 10-minute yoga flow
After yoga → I will do 20 minutes of cardio (run/bike/rowing)
After cardio → I will do 5 minutes of strength training
After strength → I will take a cold shower
After shower → I will do 10-minute meditation
After meditation → I will journal for 5 minutes
After journaling → I will review my goals and priorities
After reviewing goals → I will make a healthy breakfast
After I make breakfast → I will eat while reading for 10 minutes
After eating → I will prepare a healthy lunch for later
After preparing lunch → I will review my calendar and task list
After reviewing schedule → I will brush teeth and get dressed for day
After getting dressed → I will pack my bag and leave
```

**Total time**: ~60 minutes
**Benefits**: Deep investment in mental and physical well-being, sets powerful tone for day

### Customization Principles

**Start small**: Begin with 3-5 habit stack, expand over time

**Personalize**: Include habits that matter to YOUR goals and values

**Be specific**: "Exercise" is vague; "10 pushups" or "2-minute plank" is specific

**Front-load wins**: Put easiest/most enjoyable habits early to build momentum

**End on high note**: Final habit should feel rewarding to reinforce entire routine

## Stacking Chains: A → B → C → D

### The Cascading Effect

**Principle**: Each habit in a stack can serve as the anchor for the next, creating cascading chains of behavior.

**Notation**:
- A = Existing habit (anchor)
- B = First new habit
- C = Second new habit
- D = Third new habit
- And so on...

**Formula**:
```
After A, I will B
After B, I will C
After C, I will D
```

### Multi-Domain Example Chain

**Goal**: Morning routine touching physical, mental, and productive domains

```
A (existing): Alarm goes off
   ↓
B (new habit 1): Immediately make bed
   ↓
C (new habit 2): Do 10 pushups
   ↓
D (new habit 3): Meditate for 2 minutes
   ↓
E (new habit 4): Write top 3 priorities
   ↓
F (new habit 5): Make healthy breakfast
```

**Progressive implementation**:
- Week 1-2: Just A → B (alarm → make bed)
- Week 3-4: Add C (alarm → bed → pushups)
- Week 5-6: Add D (alarm → bed → pushups → meditate)
- Week 7-8: Add E (alarm → bed → pushups → meditate → priorities)
- Week 9-10: Add F (alarm → bed → pushups → meditate → priorities → breakfast)

**Result**: After 10 weeks, complete 5-habit morning chain running automatically

### Branching Chains

**Concept**: Chains can branch based on conditions

**Example**: Post-work routine with branches

```
After I finish work → I will close laptop
   ↓
After closing laptop → I will change clothes
   ↓
After changing clothes → IF weekday
                            ↓
                         I will prep tomorrow's lunch
                            ↓
                         I will layout tomorrow's clothes

                         IF weekend
                            ↓
                         I will plan a fun activity
                            ↓
                         I will call a friend or family member
```

### Parallel Chains

**Concept**: Multiple independent chains for different contexts

**Example**: Context-specific chains

**Morning chain**: Alarm → Bed → Pushups → Meditate → Journal

**Workday chain**: Arrive → Close tabs → Phone away → Review tasks → Start priority

**Evening chain**: Dinner → Dishes → Coffee prep → Clothes out → Read → Sleep

**Benefit**: Each chain is context-triggered, multiple powerful routines don't interfere with each other

## Common Mistakes in Habit Stacking

### Mistake 1: Using Vague Anchors

**Error**: "After I wake up, I will exercise"

**Problem**: "Wake up" is not specific enough. Do you exercise before or after bathroom? Before or after checking phone? Before or after coffee?

**Fix**: "After I make my bed, I will do 10 pushups"

### Mistake 2: Anchoring to Inconsistent Habits

**Error**: "After I check social media, I will do my language practice"

**Problem**: Social media checking is inconsistent - sometimes you don't do it, sometimes multiple times per day, timing varies wildly.

**Fix**: "After I eat breakfast, I will do my language practice for 5 minutes"

### Mistake 3: Starting New Habit Too Large

**Error**: "After I pour my coffee, I will exercise for 60 minutes"

**Problem**: 60-minute habit has too much friction, especially stacked onto simple habit like pouring coffee. Won't happen consistently.

**Fix**: "After I pour my coffee, I will do 1 minute of stretching" (can naturally expand over time)

### Mistake 4: Poor Location/Energy Matching

**Error**: "After I get into bed, I will do an intense workout"

**Problem**: Bed location and energy state don't match high-energy workout.

**Fix**: "After I get into bed, I will read one page" or move workout to different anchor: "After I pour morning coffee, I will do my workout"

### Mistake 5: Stacking Too Many Habits at Once

**Error**: Trying to implement 10-habit morning chain starting tomorrow

**Problem**: Overwhelming, all-or-nothing approach leads to abandonment when you miss one habit.

**Fix**: Start with 2-habit stack, master it for 2 weeks, then add third habit, etc.

### Mistake 6: Not Considering Frequency Alignment

**Error**: "After I brush my teeth (2x/day), I will do my weekly review"

**Problem**: Frequency mismatch - you'll do the weekly review twice per day or miss it most times.

**Fix**: "After I close my laptop on Friday afternoon, I will do my weekly review"

### Mistake 7: Creating Illogical Flow

**Error**: "After I finish my workout, I will get dressed for work"

**Problem**: Now you need to shower between these habits, breaking the stack.

**Fix**: "After I finish my workout, I will take a shower. After I shower, I will get dressed for work"

### Mistake 8: Anchoring to Variable-Duration Habits

**Error**: "After I read, I will meditate"

**Problem**: Reading could be 5 minutes or 2 hours, creating inconsistent trigger.

**Fix**: "After I read for my set 15 minutes, I will meditate" or use time-based cue: "After I set my book down at 8:30 PM, I will meditate"

### Mistake 9: No Clear Completion Point

**Error**: "After I work, I will exercise"

**Problem**: When is work "complete"? Unclear endpoint means unclear trigger.

**Fix**: "After I close my laptop and leave my desk at 5 PM, I will change into workout clothes"

### Mistake 10: Forgetting the Anchor Habit Exists

**Error**: Creating beautiful stack but realizing you don't actually do the anchor habit consistently

**Problem**: If anchor isn't reliable, entire stack collapses.

**Fix**: Honestly audit your actual daily behaviors before choosing anchors. Use only genuinely automatic habits as anchors.

## How to Troubleshoot Failed Stacks

### Diagnostic Framework

**When a habit stack isn't working, ask**:

1. Is the anchor habit actually happening consistently?
2. Am I noticing when the anchor completes?
3. Is there a clear completion point to the anchor?
4. Is the new habit too difficult/large?
5. Is there too much friction to do the new habit?
6. Is the timing/location appropriate?
7. Am I stacking too many habits at once?
8. Is there sufficient motivation for the new habit?

### Troubleshooting Process

**Step 1: Observe without judgment**
- For one week, just notice when the stack fails
- Don't try to force it, just observe
- Write down what happens at the moment of failure

**Step 2: Identify the break point**
- Where exactly does the chain break?
- Is it: Not noticing anchor, noticing but not doing new habit, starting but not finishing?

**Step 3: Categorize the failure**

**Type A: Anchor problem**
- Not noticing anchor completion
- Anchor doesn't happen consistently
- Anchor has no clear endpoint

**Type B: Transition problem**
- Notice anchor but don't transition to new habit
- Get distracted between habits
- Forget the connection

**Type C: Execution problem**
- Start new habit but don't finish
- New habit is too difficult/long
- Too much friction

**Step 4: Apply specific fix**

**For Type A (Anchor problem)**:
- Choose more obvious anchor with clear completion point
- Add physical reminder at anchor location
- Choose anchor that happens at consistent time/place

**For Type B (Transition problem)**:
- Reduce gap between anchor and new habit to zero
- Create physical connection (e.g., exercise mat next to bed for morning stack)
- Write down stack and place note where anchor happens
- Use verbal cue: say out loud "I just [ANCHOR], now I [NEW HABIT]"

**For Type C (Execution problem)**:
- Make new habit smaller (2-minute rule)
- Reduce friction (pre-position materials)
- Choose easier version of habit
- Stack at time when you have more energy

### Rebuild Strategy

**Complete stack failure**: If entire stack has fallen apart

**Recovery protocol**:
1. Don't try to restart full stack
2. Identify the core habit (most important one)
3. Create a new single stack: Anchor → Core habit only
4. Master that for 2 weeks
5. Add second habit back
6. Master for 2 weeks
7. Continue rebuilding chain one habit at a time

### Prevention Strategies

**Design stacks to be resilient**:

1. **Use micro-habits initially** (all stacked habits start tiny)
2. **Keep chains short** (3-5 habits maximum)
3. **Build in flexibility** (allow skipping non-essential habits without guilt)
4. **Create minimum viable version** (which habits are absolutely core vs. bonus?)
5. **Review and adjust monthly** (what's working? What's not? Why?)

### Adaptation Over Perfection

**Key principle**: Your stack should evolve with your life

**Signs you need to update your stack**:
- Schedule has changed (new job, new routine)
- Season has changed (morning routine in winter vs. summer)
- Life phase has changed (new baby, kids off to college, retirement)
- Interests have shifted (new goals, different priorities)
- Original habits are now automatic (can serve as anchors for new behaviors)

**Permission to modify**: A stack that evolves is better than a perfect stack that you abandon.

## LLM-ACTIONABLE-INSIGHTS

### For LLM Coaching Applications

**When helping users create habit stacks**:

1. **Begin with discovery**:
   - "What habits do you already do every single day without thinking?"
   - "Which of these happens at a consistent time and place?"
   - "Which has a clear completion point?"

2. **Match anchor to new habit**:
   - "Where will you be when [anchor] completes?"
   - "What's your energy level at that moment?"
   - "Does it make sense to do [new habit] right then?"

3. **Start small**:
   - "What's the tiniest version of [new habit] you could do right after [anchor]?"
   - "Could you make this even smaller?"
   - "Does this feel almost too easy? Good, that's the point."

4. **Test the connection**:
   - "Can you visualize finishing [anchor] and immediately doing [new habit]?"
   - "What could get in the way of that transition?"
   - "How can we remove that obstacle?"

### Pattern Recognition for LLM

**Identify automatic habits in user's narrative**:
- Listen for "I always...", "Every day I...", "Without thinking I..."
- These phrases indicate automatic behaviors = potential anchors

**Red flags in user's stack attempts**:
- Vague language: "after I wake up", "when I have time"
- Aspirational rather than actual: "after I go to the gym" (if they don't currently go)
- Too many habits: listing 10+ behaviors to stack
- No specificity: "exercise" instead of "10 squats"

### Coaching Conversation Flow

**Phase 1: Discover existing habits**
```
LLM: "Let's identify your existing automatic behaviors. What do you do every single morning without thinking about it?"

User: "I guess I make coffee, check my phone, brush teeth..."

LLM: "Perfect. Which of those happens at the exact same time and place every day?"

User: "Making coffee - I do it right when I get to the kitchen, first thing."

LLM: "Great anchor. Coffee starts brewing at a specific moment, right? After you press start, there's a perfect window while it brews. What new habit would you like to build?"
```

**Phase 2: Design the stack**
```
User: "I want to exercise more"

LLM: "Let's make this super specific. What's the smallest version of exercise you could do right after you press start on the coffee maker?"

User: "Maybe 10 pushups?"

LLM: "Even better - could you do just 1 pushup? Or just put on your workout shoes?"

User: "Just 1 pushup seems too easy..."

LLM: "Exactly - and that's why it will work. We're building the connection, not exhausting you. Once the habit is automatic, it will naturally expand. So: After I press start on the coffee maker, I will do 1 pushup. How does that feel?"
```

**Phase 3: Troubleshoot failures**
```
User: "It's not working, I keep forgetting"

LLM: "Let's diagnose this. When you press start on the coffee maker, do you notice that moment? Or does it happen on autopilot?"

User: "I guess I don't really think about it..."

LLM: "That's our issue. Let's add awareness. For the next 3 days, just say out loud 'Coffee is starting' when you press the button. Don't worry about the pushup yet. Can you do that?"

User: "Yes, that seems doable"

LLM: "After those 3 days, add: 'Coffee is starting, time for my pushup.' Then do just one. The verbal cue will bridge the gap between your automatic anchor and your new habit."
```

### Adaptive Coaching

**Track over time**:
- Which stacks succeed?
- Which anchors prove most reliable for this user?
- What time of day works best for this user?
- What friction points keep appearing?

**Adapt recommendations**:
- Suggest anchors similar to past successes
- Avoid patterns that have failed before
- Adjust complexity based on user's track record
- Celebrate wins and build on what works
